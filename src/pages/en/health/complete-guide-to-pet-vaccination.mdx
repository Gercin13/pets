Vaccination is the single most effective thing you can do to protect your pet from
preventable, often fatal disease. But between core and non-core shots, boosters, and
titer tests, the schedule can feel overwhelming. Here's the plain-language version.

## Why the timing matters

A puppy or kitten's early immunity comes from its mother's milk — protection that fades
over the first few months. Vaccines bridge that gap, and giving them **too early or too
late** leaves a dangerous window open. That's why the first year follows a tighter
schedule than adulthood.

## Core vs. non-core vaccines

**Core vaccines** protect against diseases that are widespread, severe, or transmissible
to humans — every pet should receive them. **Non-core vaccines** are recommended based on
lifestyle and region: a dog that hikes and boards needs more coverage than one that rarely
leaves the apartment.

### What to expect afterward

Mild tiredness or a little tenderness at the injection site is normal for a day. Contact
your vet promptly if you notice:

- Swelling of the face or muzzle
- Repeated vomiting or diarrhea
- Difficulty breathing

These reactions are rare, but knowing the signs means you can act fast.
